A Memory from the Past

It was a clear cold day in early February 1936. Winfield's townspeople were braving deep snow to get to church. Farmers were busy scooping to open the roads as this was before snow plows.

A lady on her way to church looked up and saw smoke coming from the roof of a house. She ran to the home and alerted the residents inside.

The fire department was called and the mother, who was confined to bed with a broken hip, was quickly evacuated north across the street to the neighboring Eichenauer place. The rest of the family, some still in their night clothes, grabbed coats and fled for their lives.

With it being so cold, the firemen fought a losing battle to save the home and soon became walking icicles.

A general alarm was put out over the country lines and men scooping snow threw down their shovels and hurried afoot across the fields to town to see if there was anything they could do to help.

The Presbyterian Church canceled services so men could volunteer.

As many of you may remember, this was the fire that destroyed the home of Mrs. Anna Wheeler, who had a son, Ross, and a daughter, Nell Sibert and her son, Bobby.

It's rumored that in the melee, Henry Marshall pitched a piano out of an upstairs window.

The house that burned down sat on the southeast corner of Locust and Center Streets. Eventually, another house was built on this property and this is where Jackie Rickey resides today.

After this fiasco, the Mayor called for a fire truck which he felt was sorely needed, but this didn't come about until 1938.

The hose cart that was used to fight this fire in 1936 is on display at the Winfield Historical Museum.

Frances Lowe
