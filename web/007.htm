Telephone Interviews:

Be Prepared

  • Prepare for a phone interview just as you would for a regular interview.
  • Compile a list of your strengths and weaknesses, as well as a list of answers to typical interview questions. In addition, be prepared for a phone conversation about your background and skills.
  • Keep your resume in clear view, so it's at your fingertips to answer questions.
  • Have a short list of your accomplishments available to review.
  • Have a pen and paper handy for note taking.
  • Turn call-waiting off so your call isn't interrupted.
  • Clear the room - evict the kids and the pets.
  • Turn off the stereo and the TV. Close the door.
  • Don't smoke, chew gum, or eat.
  • Do keep a glass of water handy.
  • Smile. Smiling will change the tone of your voice.
  • Speak slowly and enunciate clearly.
  • Use the person's title (Mr. or Ms. and their last name).
  • Don't interrupt the interviewer.
  • Take your time - take a moment or two to collect your thoughts.
  • Give short but thorough answers.
  • Remember your goal is to set-up a face-to-face interview.
  • After you thank the interviewer ask if it would be possible to meet in person.

 

Face-to-Face Interview Tips:

What to wear?

Male: Dark suit, black polished shoes, black socks, contrasting tie...or, nice slacks with white shirt, tie and polished shoes.

Female: Business suit, white/ivory blouse, matching shoes and hosiery. Avoid excessive makeup, jewelry, hairspray, perfumes or colognes. A good rule of thumb is to not wear anything too tight, too short or too revealing. The more conservative the better.

Always arrive early! At least 10 minutes. Do not smoke for two hours prior to interview.

First Impression! Body language: firm handshake, direct eye contact, sit up straight and SMILE.

Positive Mental Attitude! Stay focused on getting the offer. Any concerns can be addressed at the end of your final interview.

Be prepared: Have a written list of questions that pertain to the job expectations only. Questions regarding vacation time, time off, benefits, etc., should only be discussed if brought up by the interviewer. Usually these issues are covered prior to a formal offer.

Be Ready to answer tough questions. Role-playing or practicing your interview with someone prior can be helpful. Be prepared for such questions as:

  • Describe your background and experiences, or tell me about yourself.
  • What are your strengths? What are your weaknesses?
  • Have you ever had a dispute with a supervisor or coworker? How did you handle it?
  • What was your role in your last position?
  • Where do you expect to see yourself five years from now?
  • How do you think you will benefit this organization? How will we benefit you?
  • What about this opportunity interests you?
  • Why did you leave your last employer?
  • Who should be contacted for reference and what will they say about you?
  • Why should I hire you?

Negotiating Salary: Always let the interviewer bring up the subject of salary first. When asked for a specific dollar amount you are looking for, always try to give a range rather than a specific dollar amount. For example: "I've started positions at as little as $10.00 an hour and as much as $13.00, I would like to start somewhere in between."

Close the Interview: "I am very excited about this opportunity; I think I would be an excellent fit! Thank you for considering me, I look forward to hearing from you!"

Remember to ask for their business card in order to write a thank you note. Thank you notes should only be sent however if you are truly interested and want the position and should be sent immediately following the interview.
